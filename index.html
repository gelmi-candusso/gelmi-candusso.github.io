What I Offer

Real-World Ecological Solutions

From municipal plans to protected area management, ecological insight is essential for sustainable decision-making. I provide science-driven analyses, actionable recommendations, and training for organizations working at the human-nature interface.

🗺️
01 / Connectivity

Landscape Connectivity Analysis

Identify movement corridors, barriers, and stepping stones using GPS-tracking, remote sensing, and circuit-theory models. Applied to urban planning, infrastructure projects, and habitat restoration. Essential for 30×30 conservation targets.

🦊
02 / Coexistence

Human–Wildlife Conflict Management

Understand the ecological drivers of conflict through behavioral ecology, habitat selection analysis, and social-ecological systems frameworks. Develop evidence-based mitigation strategies that protect both communities and wildlife.

🌿
03 / Conservation

Urban Conservation Planning

Assess biodiversity and ecological function in fragmented urban landscapes. Evaluate SLOSS trade-offs and renaturalization strategies to maximize connectivity and species diversity within municipal planning constraints.

📷
04 / Monitoring

Camera Trap Network Design & Analysis

Design cost-effective wildlife monitoring programs using camera trap arrays. Build species interaction networks, estimate occupancy, and deploy computer vision (machine learning) pipelines for large-scale population monitoring.

Wildlife & Ecosystem Services

Quantify the ecological services provided by wildlife through movement ecology, genetic methods, and mechanistic modeling. Identify keystone dispersers and their vulnerability to habitat loss in urban and tropical systems.

Hands-on training in spatial ecology tools — landscape connectivity (Omniscape, Circuitscape), habitat selection models, occupancy modeling, and machine learning for wildlife data. Tailored for researchers, agencies, and NGOs.

Landscape Connectivity · Urban Predators

Dynamic Coyote Connectivity in the Greater Toronto Area

Used GPS-collar data from 27 coyotes to build dynamic habitat selection and landscape connectivity models for Toronto's urban landscape. Circuit-theory models identified vegetation corridors, riverbanks, and railway lines as movement pathways, while commercial zones emerged as connectivity barriers.

↗ Outcome: Actionable maps for corridor prioritization adopted by Toronto & Region Conservation Authority; published in Frontiers in Ecology & Environment (2024)
🏙️
Urban Planning · Multispecies

SLOSS Strategy Evaluation for the Greater Toronto Region

Modeled landscape connectivity for multiple species groups across future urban growth and renaturalization scenarios in the GTA. Evaluated Single Large or Several Small (SLOSS) habitat patch configurations to guide cost-effective conservation decisions that balance economic pressures with the 30×30 biodiversity target.

↗ Outcome: Evidence base for regional green infrastructure planning; collaboration with TRCA, Peel, York & Durham Regions; published in Journal of Applied Ecology (2025)
📷
Wildlife Monitoring · Trophic Networks

Urban Predator–Prey Networks via Camera Traps

Deployed a citywide camera trap network to reconstruct predator-prey interaction networks in Toronto's urban parks and greenways. Developed a repeatable protocol combining occupancy modeling and co-occurrence analysis to monitor how urbanization reshapes ecological communities over time.

↗ Outcome: Scalable monitoring framework published in Food Webs (2023); methodology adopted for Lincoln Park Zoo's NSF urban wildlife coexistence program
🌿
Ecosystem Services · Seed Dispersal

Coyote Seed Dispersal Services in Fragmented Urban Landscapes

Integrated GPS movement data and behavioral ecology to evaluate the seed dispersal potential of coyotes in Toronto, identifying how urbanization and anthropogenic food subsidies modify natural movement patterns and their cascading effects on plant community dynamics in fragmented habitats.

↗ Outcome: First quantification of coyote seed dispersal in a major North American city; highlights unrecognized ecosystem services of urban predators

Tropical Ecology · Population Genetics

Tamarin-Mediated Seed Dispersal in the Peruvian Amazon

Conducted multi-year fieldwork in Amazonian Peru studying two tamarin species (Saguinus mystax, Leontocebus nigrifrons) and their plant mutualists. Combined direct observation, parentage analysis, and mechanistic modeling to quantify effective seed dispersal distances and their influence on plant genetic diversity.

↗ Outcome: Two landmark publications (Molecular Ecology 2017; Ecology & Evolution 2019) establishing multi-method framework for dispersal distance estimation
🗺️
GIS & Remote Sensing · Methods

OpenStreetMap Integration for Urban Ecological Models

Developed a workflow for integrating OpenStreetMap data into habitat selection and connectivity models, replacing coarse global land-cover classifications with fine-scale urban features. Applied across the GTA to dramatically improve model accuracy for wildlife behavior prediction and road mortality risk assessment.

↗ Outcome: Open-source pipeline and methodology paper providing a replicable, globally scalable approach for urban ecologists and conservation planners

Methods & Technical Expertise

My research sits at the intersection of field ecology, quantitative biology, and data science. I work with diverse data streams — from GPS telemetry and camera trap images to remote sensing products and population genomics — and apply computational methods to extract ecological insights that matter for conservation and planning.

I build models that go beyond description: habitat selection models that predict where animals move, connectivity analyses that identify where to restore corridors, and machine learning pipelines that scale monitoring to citywide datasets.

"The questions I care about most live at the boundary of ecological science and real-world decision-making — where rigorous analysis meets the urgency of conservation in a rapidly urbanizing world."

My toolkit spans multiple programming languages and specialized ecological software, allowing me to tackle complex, multi-scale spatial problems with precision and reproducibility. All workflows are documented and shareable.

About

From the Amazon
to Urban Canopies

Dr. Tiziana A. Gelmi Candusso is an NSF-funded Postdoctoral Research Fellow at the Urban Wildlife Institute, Lincoln Park Zoo, Chicago. Her research investigates the ecology of urban wildlife communities with the goal of making cities more wildlife-inclusive and ecologically functional.

Her journey began with fieldwork in the Peruvian Amazon, studying tamarin-plant mutualistic networks and their role in maintaining tropical forest diversity. This work formed the foundation of her PhD at the University of Göttingen (2019), combining behavioral ecology, population genetics, and mechanistic modeling to understand seed dispersal.

Supported by a three-year Walter Benjamin Fellowship from the German Research Foundation (DFG), she joined the Fortin Spatial Ecology Lab at the University of Toronto, where she pivoted to understanding animal movement across urbanized landscapes — from GPS-collared coyotes navigating Toronto's fragmented green spaces to citywide camera trap networks revealing predator-prey dynamics.

Today, her research spans landscape connectivity, habitat selection, species interactions, computer vision for wildlife monitoring, and integrated socio-environmental systems for understanding human-wildlife coexistence. She collaborates actively with conservation authorities, municipalities, and parks agencies to translate science into policy.
