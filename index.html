01
Landscape connectivity · Urban planning

Dynamic Corridor Mapping for Urban Coyotes in Toronto

Using GPS telemetry from 27 collared coyotes, I built time-of-day and social-status-specific connectivity models for the City of Toronto. Results revealed riverbanks, railway corridors, and areas under power lines as critical movement routes — and identified commercial clusters as primary barriers.

Findings now inform Toronto's urban green infrastructure strategy and SLOSS renaturalization planning
🌿
02
Biodiversity strategy · Multi-species

SLOSS Assessment for Urban Wildlife Connectivity

Evaluated whether single large or several small (SLOSS) renaturalization sites better support multi-species connectivity under different urban sprawl scenarios. Smaller, widely distributed natural areas proved cost-effective for balancing economic constraints with habitat conservation.

Published in Journal of Applied Ecology (2025) — directly applicable to municipal green space allocation
📷
03
Human-wildlife coexistence · One Health

Predator–Prey Networks from Urban Camera Traps

Built bipartite predator-prey networks using two camera trap dataset types to characterize trophic interactions in urban environments. Found that combining predation events and potential encounters yields the most complete ecological network — informing wildlife management in city parks.

Method now deployable for rapid biodiversity assessment in any urban green space with camera infrastructure
🌱
04
Seed dispersal · Behavioral ecology

Frugivore Behavior and Plant Genetic Structure in Neotropical Forests

PhD research at the German Primate Center combining GPS tracking of tamarin monkeys with plant parentage analysis to estimate true seed dispersal distances. Compared four estimation methods to resolve discrepancies between behavioral observation and genetic evidence.

Framework for integrating movement ecology and molecular genetics now applied in urban zoochory contexts
About

Tiziana Gelmi-Candusso

I am a spatial ecologist specializing in the intersection of urban ecology, wildlife behaviour, and landscape analysis. My research bridges the gap between academic ecological science and real-world conservation and planning challenges.

Currently a Research Fellow at the Urban Wildlife Institute at Lincoln Park Zoo, I work on understanding how wildlife communities navigate—and sometimes thrive in—human-dominated landscapes. Previously, I was hosted by the Fortin Lab at the University of Toronto.

My toolbox spans GPS telemetry analysis, circuit-theory connectivity modelling, hierarchical Bayesian occupancy models, computer vision, and agent-based simulation — always applied in service of actionable outcomes.
